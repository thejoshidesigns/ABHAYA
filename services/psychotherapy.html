What supportive psychotherapy is

Supportive psychotherapy is a form of talk therapy that pairs a warm, consistent therapeutic relationship with practical, evidence-based skills. It is not the same as psychoanalysis or long-term exploratory work - though it can include those elements when useful.

The goal is straightforward: help you feel better, function better, and understand yourself more clearly. Sessions are collaborative. You set the agenda; your therapist helps you make sense of what's happening, learn new skills, and move toward what matters to you.

Who it helps

Supportive psychotherapy is a good fit for a wide range of concerns, including:

  • Depression and persistent low mood
  • Anxiety, panic, and chronic worry
  • Stress related to work, relationships, or major life changes
  • Grief and loss
  • Low self-esteem and self-criticism
  • Difficulty managing emotions or impulses
  • Adjustment to a new diagnosis, medication, or life stage
What to expect

Your first session

  1. 01

    Getting to know you

    The first session is a conversation. We'll talk about what brought you in, what you've tried before, what you're hoping for, and what kind of life you'd like to be living. No pressure to perform or to "have it figured out."

  2. 02

    Your goals

    By the end of the first session, you should have a clear sense of whether this is a good fit, and we'll talk together about what a treatment plan might look like - frequency, focus areas, and whether medication should be part of the picture.

  3. 03

    Session format

    Sessions are typically 45 or 60 minutes, and most patients meet weekly or every other week at the start. As you build skills and stability, we often space sessions out gradually. Both in-person and secure telepsychiatry are available.

How we work

Approaches we use

We draw from several evidence-based approaches, choosing what fits the person in front of us rather than following a single school of thought.

CBT

Cognitive behavioral therapy - for depression, anxiety, and thought patterns that keep you stuck.

DBT-informed

Skills work for emotion regulation, distress tolerance, and interpersonal effectiveness.

Mindfulness

For grounding, attention, and a kinder relationship with your own experience.

Questions

Frequently asked questions about therapy

How long does therapy take?

It depends on what you're working on. Some concerns resolve in 8–12 sessions; others benefit from longer-term work. We'll check in regularly about how it's going and adjust as needed. You're never locked in.

Do I have to come every week?

Weekly is standard at the start, but biweekly or monthly sessions work well for many people - especially once you've built skills. We'll figure out a cadence that fits your life and your goals.

Can I do therapy over telehealth?

Yes. Therapy translates very well to secure video for most concerns. Some people prefer in-person and that's fine too - we offer both.

Do I need to be in medication management to do therapy here?

No. You can do therapy, medication management, or both - your choice. Many patients do both, but plenty see us for one service only.
